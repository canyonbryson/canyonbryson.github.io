Projects Description

E. Coli Transformation Protein Isolation Tensile Testing

We first used chemical competence to transform E. Coli with an orange flourescent protein and ampicillin resistance. We then grew the colonies on agar plates. Then, we cultured the colonies in a homemade bioreacter, using a mason jar and some arduino code, as seen set up in the picture on the far left. The result produced orange bacteria that could bee seen without UV light, as seen in the second picture, jar on the far left. We then lyced the cells with transglutaminase and poured it in a dogbone mold, as seen in the third picture. We then tested its tensile strength.

In other words, we made bacteria glow orange, then let it grow, then poured it in a dogbone mold and tested it's 'stretchiness.'

"PTC" PCR Gel Electrophoresis

We amplified our own DNA using PCR, then cut it with a restriction digest, and analyzed through Gel Electrophoresis to determine if we contained the gene to taste PTC.

Streaking E. Coli

From a glycerol stock, I streaked E. Coli with the OFP Plasmid with AMP resistance. If accepted, this would allow the E. Coli to glow orange and survive in ampicillin. However, I streaked the E. Coli on a regular LB Plate with no ampicillin. Thus, the E. Coli had no reason to accept the plasmid, as they could live without it, they choose not to expend the unnecessary effort. Normally, they would need the AMP resistance to survive in a plate with ampicillin, so they would take the plasmid, resulting in them also taking the orange protein gene.

Biological Engineering

Biosynthetic Engineering

Biosynthetic engineering is synthesizing natural or biological products, through methods such as genetic engineering. For example, the E. Coli Project in my BENG 1000 class. We transformed E. Coli with an orange flourescent protein and AMP resistance, then grew the E. Coli on an agar plate with ampicillin, so the bacteria had to use the gene or die. Basically, if you want bacteria to do anything, you have to threaten to kill it first. Survival of the fittest.

This process involves the introduction of a plasmid into the bacterial cell. A plasmid is a small, circular piece of DNA. The plasmid contains a promotor sequence, to start the transcription (gene expression) process. Then it contains the desired gene, from which the desire protein will be translated. Then followed by a termination sequence to end the transcription process. The plasmid also contains an antibiotic resistance gene.

One application of biosynthetic engineering is in recombinant DNA technology. Because DNA uses a universal code, the same sequence can be read and understood by any organism. For example, the human gene for insulin production can be given to E. Coli, which will then produce insulin. This has extensive applications for personalized healthcare.

Bioprocess Engineering

This deals with the production and manufacturing of biological products. For example, in the E. Coli Project in my BENG 1000 class, after we synthesized our bacteria with the desired protein, we built bioreactors to facilitate bacterial growth in suspension, producing the bacteria large-scale.

The first step in biological engineering is to be able to synthesize the desired product through biosynthetic engineering. However, for it to have an impact, it must be produced in large scale. For example, if you synthesized a new medicine, it must be cheap to produce on a large scale, otherwise no one would feasibly have access to the medicine.

Current research include how cell therapy and regenerative medicine can impact burn victims through stem cell differentiation and skin cell transplantation. Further advances in bioprocess engineering and adnvanced biomanufacturing will allow the implementation of many new therapies.

Biomedical Engineering

Biomedical engineering includes the development of prosthetics, medical devices, drug delivery, wearable health monitors, synthesizing tissue, bone, or organs with biomaterials, biodevices, and anything else in biological engineering with medical applications.

In the E. Coli Project, we created a dogbone mold from lysed E. Coli cells with orange flourescent proteins, then tested it's tensile strength. This served as an introduction to the possibility of synthesizing tissue with the desired proteins. This could potentially be 3-d "bioprinted" one day into a durable structure compatible with the human body.

Current research includes a microelectronic implant in the eye, which has shown to give a blind person rudimentary vision. Current versions allow for basic navigation and even letter reading, while future models will further improve vision for face recognition and more.

Bioenvironmental Engineering

Bioenvironmental engineering involves the treatment of waste for renewed energy, preserving energy and reducing energy consumption, and producing alternate forms of energy. This can include bioagriculture, or engineering plants to maximize output and food quality.

Current research includes forming a new way to turn methane into electricity. Microbial fuel cells use bacteria to convert methane into electricity. These bacteria consume the methane, leaving extra electrons that can be harvested and used for electricity.

I am working with Dr. Simms and a few other students on the Mesobiliverdin Project to grow and harvest green algae, which contain cyanobacteria (often harmful), but this specific strand can be converted into mesobiliverdin, an antioxidant with medical applications.
